How to Choose a Radon Mitigation Contractor in Cedar Rapids

What to look for, questions to ask, and red flags to avoid when hiring a radon professional.

March 1, 2026

Hiring the right radon mitigation contractor is one of the most important decisions you will make as a homeowner dealing with elevated radon levels. A properly installed system will reduce radon to safe levels and last for decades with minimal maintenance. A poorly installed system can fail to reduce levels adequately, create noise problems, or even cause moisture issues in your home.

Here is what every Cedar Rapids homeowner should know before hiring a radon mitigation contractor.

Look for Proper Certifications and Licensing

The single most important qualification to verify is whether the contractor holds a current certification from the National Radon Proficiency Program (NRPP) or the National Radon Safety Board (NRSB). These are the two nationally recognized credentialing organizations for radon professionals. Certified mitigators have completed required coursework, passed proficiency exams, and agreed to follow industry standards for system design and installation.

In Iowa, the Iowa Department of Public Health maintains a registry of certified radon measurement and mitigation providers. You can verify a contractor's credentials through the IDPH website or by asking the contractor directly for their certification number. Any reputable contractor will provide this information without hesitation.

Beyond radon-specific certification, make sure your contractor carries general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. This protects you from liability if a worker is injured on your property and covers damage to your home if something goes wrong during installation. Ask for a certificate of insurance and verify that the policy is current.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring

A good radon contractor will welcome your questions and provide clear, detailed answers. Here are the essential questions to ask during the estimate process:

How many systems have you installed in the Cedar Rapids area?

Local experience matters significantly in radon mitigation. A contractor who has installed hundreds of systems in Cedar Rapids and Linn County will understand the local soil conditions, common home construction types, and specific challenges that come with mitigating homes in our area. The glacial till soils in eastern Iowa behave differently than the sandy soils found in other parts of the country, and this affects system design decisions.

Do you guarantee radon levels will be reduced below 4 pCi/L?

Any experienced contractor should offer a written guarantee that the system will reduce radon levels below the EPA action level of 4 pCi/L. Most qualified contractors go further, aiming for levels below 2 pCi/L. If a contractor is unwilling to guarantee results, that is a significant red flag. A properly designed and installed sub-slab depressurization system achieves levels below 4 pCi/L in the vast majority of Cedar Rapids homes.

Is post-mitigation testing included?

Post-mitigation testing is the only way to verify that the system is actually working. This should be a non-negotiable part of any radon mitigation contract. The test should be conducted at least 24 hours after the system is activated and should follow EPA measurement protocols. Some contractors include this testing in their installation price, while others charge separately. Either way, make sure it is part of the agreement before work begins.

What type of fan will you install, and what is the warranty?

The radon fan is the heart of the mitigation system, and quality matters. Ask about the brand and model of fan the contractor plans to use. Reputable brands include RadonAway, Festa, and Fantech. Most quality fans come with a 5-year manufacturer warranty, and some contractors offer additional labor warranties on the installation itself. A fan that fails prematurely will allow radon levels to climb back up, so investing in a quality fan is important.

Will you provide a written estimate and scope of work?

Before any work begins, you should receive a written estimate that clearly outlines the scope of work, the type of system being installed, the number of suction points, the fan model, the pipe routing, and the total cost. This document protects both you and the contractor by establishing clear expectations. Verbal estimates and handshake agreements are not sufficient for a project that directly affects your family's health.

Red Flags to Watch For

Not every contractor who offers radon mitigation services is equally qualified. Here are warning signs that should make you look elsewhere:

No Written Guarantee

If a contractor is unwilling to guarantee in writing that radon levels will be reduced below 4 pCi/L, walk away. This is a standard industry practice, and any qualified contractor should stand behind their work. A lack of guarantee suggests either inexperience or a lack of confidence in their installation quality.

No Post-Mitigation Testing

A contractor who installs a system and leaves without testing is cutting a critical corner. Without post-mitigation testing, you have no way to verify the system is working. Some unscrupulous contractors skip this step because they know their installation may not adequately reduce levels. Always insist on documented post-mitigation test results.

No Written Estimate or Contract

Any contractor who refuses to provide a detailed written estimate before starting work is not someone you want in your home. A written estimate protects you from unexpected charges and ensures both parties agree on the scope of work. It should include the total price, what is included, the timeline for completion, and warranty information.

Significantly Below-Market Pricing

While competitive pricing is normal, a quote that is dramatically lower than other estimates should raise questions. In the Cedar Rapids area, a standard residential radon mitigation system typically costs between $800 and $1,500. A quote of $400 or $500 may indicate the contractor is cutting corners on materials, using a lower-quality fan, skipping slab sealing, or not including post-mitigation testing.

Pressure to Decide Immediately

A reputable contractor will give you time to review their estimate, compare it with other quotes, and make an informed decision. High-pressure sales tactics, including claims that you must act immediately or face dire consequences, are not appropriate. While radon is a serious health concern and you should not delay indefinitely, a few days to compare estimates is perfectly reasonable.

Why Local Experience in Iowa Matters

Radon mitigation is not a one-size-fits-all process. The effectiveness of a system depends heavily on local soil conditions, foundation construction, and climate. In Cedar Rapids and the broader Linn County area, several factors make local experience particularly valuable:

  • Glacial till soils: Eastern Iowa's glacial till can have varying permeability, which affects how easily air moves beneath the slab. An experienced local contractor knows how to test for sub-slab communication and adjust system design accordingly.
  • Basement construction: Many Cedar Rapids homes feature full basements with poured concrete or block walls. Local contractors understand the common crack patterns, sump pit configurations, and drain tile systems found in these homes.
  • Winter conditions: Iowa's harsh winters mean the system must be designed to prevent moisture condensation and ice buildup in the exhaust pipe. Proper pipe insulation and routing are essential, and a local contractor will know from experience where problems are likely to occur.
  • Building codes: Local building codes and permit requirements can vary. A contractor who regularly works in Cedar Rapids will be familiar with any applicable local regulations and ensure the installation is compliant.

Insurance Requirements

Before allowing any contractor to begin work on your home, verify that they carry both general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. General liability insurance protects you if the contractor damages your home during installation. Workers' compensation covers medical expenses and lost wages if a worker is injured on your property.

Ask the contractor for a certificate of insurance and verify directly with the insurance company that the policy is current and in good standing. Do not simply take the contractor's word for it. A contractor who operates without proper insurance puts you at significant financial risk.
